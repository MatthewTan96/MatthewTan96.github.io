A Game of Monster Bishops

This game was interesting because it occurred right after my brother asked me if a bishop was stronger than a knight to which I answered "that purely depends on the position of the board". After that I showed him this game.

To answer the question, bishops are stronger in "Open Positions", where the pawns are free to move around and are not locked in place. A bishop can travel across the board while a knight cannot. A bishop can guard all advancing squares of the knight, making it stronger if fully mobile. This game shows an example of a monster bishop pair, and how I forced the opponent to trade a knight for a bishop. Be sure to check my notes as you scroll through the game!

Annotations and Notes

3. Qh5 is typically considered a very amateurish move. The aim of that is to try and achieve #Qxf7 checkmate. ... e6 is the only way to stop checkmate and defend the c5 pawn.

10. Qe4 shows that my opponent is willing to trade a bishop for a knight, a trade he will soon regret. The pushed pawns are blocked (e6 and c5), but they can be opened quickly. A thought that never occured to him at the time.

12. Qh4 loses a pawn as shown in the subsequent moves.

14. Nf3+ forces a trade of a knight for a bishop. No matter he the opponent recaptures (gxf3 or Nxf3) I will take his bishop (Bxb2) with tempo as I am attacking his rook at the same time. ... gxf3 is a weaker move than Nxf3, as his knight is stuck in the corner with almost no squares to go to.

22. Rxe8+ Remember! If you position is losing, never trade! However this guy keeps doing it!

28. Nf4 loses his rook, as I have a discovered attack on his rook as you will see in the next moves.

30. ... Ra1+ forces the king off the protection of the knight, and he loses yet another piece. Game over.
